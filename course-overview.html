Introduction

Welcome to the Medcom Prompting and assisting with medication in Home Care course. This module gives you essential knowledge for safe, effective prompting and assisting with medication in home care in the UK. For a deeper course on administration, use Medcom Medication Administration (or your employer’s equivalent).

This course is CPD accredited, aligns with the relevant NICE guidelines, and meets the Skills for Care requirement for training in managing medication.

Who is this course for?

Suitable for anyone in health or social care needing an overview of medicines management—particularly care assistants; also relevant for nurses and senior care assistants with delegated administration duties.

Home Care includes care in the service user’s home—also called domiciliary or live-in care.

What this course is not

Most care workers may not administer medication. This is a theory module only—you must complete practical training and be assessed competent before administering. Estimated time: 20–30 minutes.

By the end of this course, you will be able to

  1. Explain prompting and assisting with medication and how this differs from medication administration.
  2. Explain the role of a care assistant in medication support in home care within the limits of your role.
  3. Recognise when a service user may require prompting or assistance within the limits of your role.
  4. Recognise possible signs of side-effects and adverse drug reactions.
  5. Identify appropriate responses if a service user refuses their medication.
  6. Use effective communication when discussing medicines with service users.
  7. Explain the importance of accurate medication records and documentation.
  8. Identify consequences of working outside the limitations of your role.

2 — What are your responsibilities?

This chapter explains your role when prompting or assisting with medication in home care, how this differs from full administration where not permitted, and the importance of following the care plan and local policy.

3 — What is prompting and assisting with medication?

Learn the definitions of prompting and assisting, when each applies, and how to support independence while keeping people safe.

4 — Why may a person need assistance?

Explore physical, cognitive, sensory, and environmental reasons someone may need help with their medicines at home.

5 — Understanding the needs of service users

Person-centred approaches, communication, capacity, consent, and respecting preferences in medication support.

6 — How is medication stored in home care?

Safe storage, security, temperature, original packaging, and reducing errors in the home environment.

7 — What are adverse drug reactions (ADR) and drug interactions?

Recognising ADRs, reporting concerns, and understanding interactions between medicines, food, and conditions.

8 — What about refusals?

What to do if a person refuses medication, escalation, documentation, and when to seek advice.

9 — Documentation and record keeping

Accurate, timely records; MAR charts; reporting; and legal/regulatory expectations in home care.

10 — Ordering, transporting, storing, and disposing

Safe processes across the medicines pathway, including waste and disposal of unused or expired medicines.

11 — Ethical considerations

Dignity, confidentiality, advocacy, and working within your role and competence.

12 — Simple scenarios

Apply learning to realistic home care situations and decision points.

13 — Summary

Key takeaways from the module before you move to review and assessment.

14 — Review & Reflect

Consolidate learning and reflect on how you will apply it in practice.

15 — 🏆 What to expect from the assessment

How the end-of-course assessment works, what is covered, and tips for success.
